In NYC, Black Children are 49% More Likely to be in Foster Care than Hispanic Children

By Katrina Ventura

It has been more than 20 years since child services came knocking on Shanene Bryant’s home in the Bronx to take away her two children. “I tried my best to hold it together,” said Bryant. “I didn’t want my children to know and just said, ‘You’re going with Grandma’.”

Bryant, 45, now works at Rise Magazine, an organization of parents impacted by the family policing system who offer support to other parents who are trying to gain custody of their children placed in foster care. She was one of many advocates representing impacted parents at the public hearing on the child welfare system and the mandatory reporting of child abuse of maltreatment. Among the concerns raised were the racial and ethnic disparities in the mandatory reporting of children in need of protection.

According to ACS Commissioner Jess Dannhauser, a Black child was 6.7 times more likely than a White child to be subject in a child protection investigation.While a Hispanic or Latinx child was 4.7 times more likely than a White child to be subject in a child protection investigation.

However, analysis of child abuse reports from the Administration for Children's Services in 2022 reveals that Black children have 1.49 times higher odds of ending up in foster care compared to Hispanic children.

A recent report by the New York Civil Liberties Union argued that there is racism at every stage of the child welfare system, noting that more Black families are reported on than White families.

A child enters the welfare system when a report to the Statewide Central Register of Child Abuse and Maltreatment (SCR) is made by a mandated reporter. Mandated reporters include professionals such as doctors, nurses, teachers, police officers, and child care center workers to report suspected child abuse and neglect to the state hotline. This practice has come under scrutiny by some child advocates due to concerns about potential bias in reporting, particularly concerning certain races or ethnicities.

ACS data for 2022 show a total of 92,305 SCR reports were made that year. Hispanic children made up 41,247 reports while 34,923 of child abuse reports were Black children. Early steps in the child welfare system determine which cases are handled as a Collaborative Assessment, Response, Engagement and Support (CARES) program, or deemed unsubstantiated by Child Protective Services. The remaining reports are then indicated for investigation.

In 2022, 27% of child abuse reports in New York City were indicated for investigation and less than 3% of these children ended up in foster care. More Hispanic children than Black children are reported at the initial stage of the child welfare system, but as each stage progresses the difference between the two groups becomes more apparent.



At the initial stages, there's no significant difference between Black and Hispanic children in terms of indicated and unsubstantiated investigations. However, a significant disparity emerges between Black and Hispanic children at later stages, particularly in the transition to foster care, indicating systemic biases.

Results of the chi-square test show a significant disparity between the number of Black and Hispanic child abuse reports being handled through the alternative CARES program, where 12% of Hispanic cases end up in CARES compared to 11% of Black cases.

The difference becomes more pronounced when the same test was conducted to compare foster care entries where 1,197 Black children in New York ended up in foster care compared to 947 Hispanic children. In this test the difference was drastic, with a p-value approaching zero, suggesting that Black and Hispanic children have vastly different rates of placement into foster care.

Aside from race, poverty was seen to have influenced the number of child abuse reports in a certain area. For this, the story examined the distribution of child abuse reports or SCR intakes in each Community District in New York City and factored in the percent poverty of households with children based on data from the Institute for Children, Poverty, and Homelessness.

Initial analysis showed that community districts with higher percentages of poverty tend to get more child abuse reports, with more concentration in the Bronx such as Mott Haven to Melrose, Hunts Point to Longwood, Morrisania to Crotona, and Belmont to East Tremont. But the biggest outlier appeared to be Brooklyn Community District 16 in Brownsville, wherein more than a third of households with children were in poverty, and over 1,300 child abuse reports were made in 2022.

The final statistical tool used in this story was multivariable regression which incorporated data such as percent of households with children living in poverty, percent of Black households, and percent of Hispanic households. The goal of running the model is to see which of the variables greatly influence the number of child abuse reports that a community district receives.

The multivariable regression showed that the percentage of Black households is the strongest predictor of the increase or decrease in child abuse reports. The percent of child abuse reports in a community district increases by approximately 0.007655 for every one-unit increase in the percent of Black households, holding other variables constant.

The reverse is true when accounting for the percentage of households with children living in poverty and the percentage of White households in the community district. Where higher percentages of households with children in poverty are associated with higher percentages of child abuse reports, while higher percentages of White households are associated with lower percentages of child abuse reports.

While poverty appeared to be a good predictor of the likelihood of a district receiving more child abuse reports, the community's racial or ethnic composition has a stronger influence on whether an area will have higher or lower rates of such reports.

The New York State Office of Children and Family Services said it has begun offering training to mandated reporters to address those biases.

“We are really trying to educate our mandated reporters on what they are obligated to do in order to protect the health and safety of children and what they are not obligated to do, which is judge against poverty and other biases that they may have,” said OCFS Acting Commissioner Suzanne Miles-Gustave.

After five years, the court granted Bryant's children.

She has won her battle with substance abuse and now works as a Parent Advocate Director for Rise where she provides support to parents who had their children taken away by ACS.

When asked how she felt about the child welfare system, Bryant said it would have been better if the ACS supported her instead of removing her children.

“They could have helped me with services and home to keep my families together instead of taking that support and traumatizing them,” said Bryant. “So it was a rescue but it was also pain.”
